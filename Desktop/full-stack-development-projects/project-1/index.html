The Hungarian Revolution of 1956

In October 1956, the people of Hungary rose against Soviet domination demanding independence and democracy. It was a brief, yet key defining moment in European history and the Cold War. Learn more about the causes, key moments, and the lasting effects of the revolution.

Why the Revolution Matters

In October 1956, Hungarian students and workers rose up against Soviet control, demanding freedom, democracy, and the withdrawal of Soviet troops. What began as a peaceful protest soon escalated into a nationwide uprising that shook the Communist bloc and inspired movements across Europe. Though the revolution was crushed within weeks, its legacy lived on as a symbol of resistance and national pride.

The Hungarian Revolution was one of the first major challenges to Soviet control in Eastern Europe. Though the uprising was crushed by Soviet tanks, its spirit lived on. It inspired later movements across the Eastern Bloc and contributed to the eventuall fall of Communism in 1989.

Key events

23 Oct 1956

Students march through Budapest with demands for reform.

25 Oct 1956

Soviet troops open fire in Kossuth Square.

4 November 1956

The Red Army invades to suppress the uprising.

22 November 1956

Rebel leaders, including Imre Nagy, are arrested.

June 1958

Imre Nagy is executed after a secret trial.
